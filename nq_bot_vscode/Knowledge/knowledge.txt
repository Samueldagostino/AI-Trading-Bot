SECTION 0: CORE DESIGN PHILOSOPHY

0.1 Foundational Axioms

This knowledge database exists under three non-negotiable axioms that override all other design considerations. Every table, every metric, and every signal pathway must satisfy all three simultaneously or be discarded.

Axiom 1 — Reality-First, Not Story-First: The bot does not trade narratives. It trades measurable, statistically validated inefficiencies. If a concept cannot be reduced to a falsifiable numeric signal with a defined edge, it does not belong in the system. No exceptions.

Axiom 2 — Survival Precedes Profit: The system’s primary objective is capital preservation under adversarial conditions. Profit is a byproduct of surviving long enough for positive expectancy to compound. Every module must answer: “How does this prevent catastrophic loss?” before “How does this generate return?”

Axiom 3 — Modularity & Isolation: Every knowledge domain operates as an independent, testable subsystem. No circular dependencies. No hidden coupling. If one module fails or degrades, it emits a confidence decay signal and the system degrades gracefully, never catastrophically.

0.2 Anti-Patterns Explicitly Rejected

Curve-fitting disguised as optimization. Narrative bias masquerading as analysis. Untested conviction treated as signal. Correlated signals counted as independent confirmation. Backtests without transaction costs, slippage, and latency modeling. Sharpe ratios calculated without regime segmentation. Any metric that cannot be computed in real time with production-grade latency.

SECTION 1: MARKET MICROSTRUCTURE

Market microstructure is the foundational layer of the knowledge database. It provides the ground-truth signal about what participants are actually doing, as opposed to what price charts retrospectively depict. Every downstream module depends on the integrity of this data.

1.1 Order Flow Imbalance

Definition: The net difference between aggressive buying volume (trades executed at or above the ask) and aggressive selling volume (trades executed at or below the bid) at each price level within a defined time window.

Why It Matters: Order flow imbalance is the highest-fidelity signal of real-time directional conviction. Unlike lagging indicators derived from OHLC candles, delta reveals who is paying the spread to initiate positions now. Persistent imbalance at a price level indicates institutional urgency. Divergence between price direction and cumulative delta is a statistically significant reversal precursor in NQ futures.

Metrics: Bid/ask volume delta per bar, cumulative delta (session and multi-session), footprint imbalance ratio (aggressive buy volume ÷ aggressive sell volume per price level), delta divergence index (price making new highs while cumulative delta declines, or inverse), absorption ratio (large resting orders absorbing aggressive flow without price movement).

Critical Implementation Note: Delta must be computed from tick-level trade data with accurate aggressor-side classification. Using volume-based approximations from OHLC bars introduces systematic error that invalidates downstream signals. The system must use CME Level II data or equivalent.

1.2 Liquidity Topology

Definition: The three-dimensional mapping of resting order density across price, time, and order size. This includes visible limit orders in the book, inferred stop-loss clusters, and dark pool activity signatures.

Why It Matters: Institutional participants require liquidity to execute size. They systematically target price levels with concentrated resting orders (stops, limits, options hedging thresholds) to fill large positions with minimal slippage. Understanding where liquidity is clustered provides forward-looking insight into probable price targets.

Metrics: Order book depth heatmap (time-weighted bid/ask volume at each price level), stop-loss cluster probability (inferred from equal highs/lows, round numbers, recent swing points), prior-session high/low sweep probability, volume-at-price concentration index, dark pool print frequency by price level, options gamma exposure by strike (GEX).

1.3 Spread & Execution Quality

Definition: The real cost of entering and exiting positions, measured as the difference between the theoretical mid-price at signal generation and the actual fill price achieved, inclusive of spread, slippage, partial fills, and latency cost.

Why It Matters: In NQ scalping and short-duration trades, execution quality is often the difference between a profitable and unprofitable system. A strategy with 2-tick average expectancy is destroyed by 1.5-tick average slippage. Execution cost must be modeled as a first-class variable in all backtests and live performance attribution.

Metrics: Time-weighted average spread, implementation shortfall (signal price vs fill price), fill rate by order type (limit vs market), queue position probability for limit orders, latency distribution (signal generation to exchange acknowledgment), partial fill ratio, market impact estimate per contract size.

1.4 Market Depth Dynamics

Definition: Real-time analysis of how the order book changes in response to price movement and aggressive order flow. Measures order book resilience, spoofing signatures, and liquidity withdrawal patterns.

Why It Matters: Static order book snapshots are misleading because a large percentage of visible liquidity is pulled before execution. The rate at which liquidity refills after aggressive trades (book resilience) is a leading indicator of directional continuation or exhaustion.

Metrics: Book resilience score (time to refill consumed levels), order-to-trade ratio (OTR) per price level, layering/spoofing detection score (rapid add/cancel patterns exceeding statistical norms), depth imbalance ratio (bid depth vs ask depth within N ticks of mid), liquidity vacuum detection (sudden depth withdrawal preceding moves).

1.5 Tick-Level Trade Classification

Definition: Accurate classification of each trade execution as buyer-initiated or seller-initiated, using the Lee-Ready algorithm enhanced with order book state at the microsecond of execution.

Why It Matters: Incorrect aggressor classification cascades errors through the entire order flow analysis pipeline. The standard Lee-Ready tick test misclassifies 15-25% of NQ trades during high volatility. Enhanced classification using contemporaneous book state reduces error to below 5%.

Metrics: Classification confidence score per trade, misclassification rate estimate (backtested against known institutional flows), trade size distribution by aggressor side, block trade detection threshold, iceberg order inference probability.

SECTION 2: PRICE ACTION STRUCTURES

Price action structures encode the footprint of institutional activity into observable chart geometry. The system treats these not as mystical patterns but as statistically measurable inefficiencies with quantifiable fill probabilities, reaction strengths, and decay rates.

2.1 Fair Value Gaps (FVG)

Definition: A price imbalance created when a strong directional candle leaves a gap between the high of the candle two bars prior and the low of the current candle (bullish FVG), or the inverse (bearish FVG). This gap represents a zone where one side had zero effective resistance.

Quantified Edge: FVGs are not traded on pattern recognition alone. Each FVG is scored by: gap size relative to ATR (gaps < 0.3 ATR have low significance), fill probability distribution (time-decayed, with most fills occurring within 4-8 bars), overlap with VWAP deviation bands, and order flow confirmation at the gap boundary.

Metrics: Gap size (ticks and ATR-normalized), fill probability by time window, partial vs full fill ratio, reaction magnitude at gap boundary (price velocity on contact), institutional order flow confirmation at gap entry, FVG-to-order-block overlap score.

2.2 Order Blocks

Definition: The last opposing candle before an impulsive move that breaks structure. In institutional terms, the consolidation zone where smart money accumulated or distributed before the displacement move.

Quantified Edge: Order blocks are validated only when accompanied by a volume spike exceeding 1.5x the 20-bar volume average, the subsequent displacement move exceeds 2x ATR, and the retest shows order flow confirmation (delta divergence or absorption). Unvalidated order blocks are noise.

Metrics: Zone dimensions (price range and time duration), volume spike magnitude at formation, displacement move magnitude, retest success rate (segmented by regime), reaction strength at retest (measured in ticks and time), order block age decay (probability of holding decreases with each failed retest), confluence score with FVG and liquidity levels.

2.3 Liquidity Sweeps & Stop Hunts

Definition: Price movements that penetrate obvious support/resistance or swing points to trigger clustered stop orders, followed by a rapid reversal as the initiating institution fills against the triggered liquidity.

Quantified Edge: Sweep quality is measured by: penetration distance beyond the level (deep sweeps > 3 ticks are more significant), reversal velocity (the speed of price return to the pre-sweep range), delta divergence at the sweep apex (aggressive sellers at a low sweep while cumulative delta is rising indicates absorption), and the relationship to session timing (NY open sweeps carry more weight).

Metrics: Sweep distance (ticks beyond level), reversal velocity (ticks per second in the 30 seconds post-sweep), delta divergence score at apex, volume spike at sweep level, sweep-to-reversal time, false sweep ratio (sweeps that do not produce meaningful reversal), sweep source classification (prior session high/low, equal highs/lows, round number).

2.4 Break of Structure (BOS) & Change of Character (CHOCH)

Definition: BOS occurs when price takes out a previous swing high (bullish) or swing low (bearish) in the direction of the prevailing trend. CHOCH occurs when this break happens against the prevailing trend, signaling a potential reversal.

Metrics: Break magnitude (ticks beyond swing point), volume confirmation at break, candle close beyond level (vs wick-only), displacement velocity, multi-timeframe alignment (does the break confirm on higher timeframes), follow-through rate (percentage of BOS/CHOCH events that produce continuation of at least 1 ATR).

2.5 Premium & Discount Zones

Definition: Relative pricing zones defined by the equilibrium midpoint of the current swing range. Premium zone is the upper half (above equilibrium), discount zone is the lower half. Institutional buyers accumulate in discount, distribute in premium.

Metrics: Current price position relative to swing equilibrium (percentile), volume distribution between premium and discount zones, delta imbalance between zones, time spent in each zone, mean reversion probability from extreme premium/discount readings.

2.6 Displacement & Imbalance Candles

Definition: Candles with body-to-range ratio exceeding 70% and range exceeding 1.5x ATR, indicating strong institutional conviction and directional urgency.

Metrics: Body-to-range ratio, range as ATR multiple, volume relative to 20-bar average, delta magnitude, follow-through probability within 4 bars, failure rate (percentage that fully retrace within session).

SECTION 3: STATISTICAL & VOLATILITY ANALYSIS

Statistical rigor separates institutional systems from retail noise. This domain provides the quantitative backbone for regime classification, position sizing, signal filtering, and performance validation.

3.1 VWAP & Deviation Bands

Definition: Volume-Weighted Average Price represents the true average transaction price for the session, weighted by volume. Standard deviation bands (1σ, 2σ, 3σ) provide statistically derived support/resistance zones that institutional algorithms use as execution benchmarks.

Metrics: Distance from VWAP (ticks and σ-normalized), VWAP slope (rate of change per bar), reversion probability by σ distance, multi-day anchored VWAP levels (weekly, monthly VWAP), VWAP cross frequency and direction, developing VWAP vs prior session VWAP divergence.

3.2 ATR & Volatility Regime Classification

Definition: Average True Range measures the expected price movement per bar. Volatility regime classification categorizes the current market state into distinct behavioral modes that determine strategy selection and parameter adjustment.

Regime Taxonomy: Low Volatility Compression (ATR < 25th percentile, ADX < 20, Hurst > 0.6): Range-bound mean reversion environment. Trending (ATR 25th-75th percentile, ADX > 25, Hurst > 0.55): Directional moves with pullback opportunities. High Volatility Expansion (ATR > 75th percentile, ADX > 30): Fast directional moves, wide stops required. Chaotic/Unstable (ATR > 90th percentile, ADX < 20, Hurst < 0.45): No edge, system should reduce or halt trading.

Metrics: 14-period ATR and percentile rank (rolling 60-day), ATR rate of change, ADX and DI+/DI- spread, Hurst exponent (rolling 100-bar), GARCH(1,1) conditional volatility, realized vs implied volatility spread, volatility term structure (intraday vs daily vs weekly ATR ratios), regime transition probability matrix.

3.3 Distribution Analysis

Definition: Characterization of return distributions including fat tails, skewness, and kurtosis that deviate from Gaussian assumptions. Most risk models catastrophically underestimate tail risk because they assume normality.

Metrics: Rolling skewness (50-bar, 200-bar), rolling kurtosis, tail ratio (mean gain of top 5% returns vs mean loss of bottom 5%), maximum adverse excursion (MAE) distribution, jump diffusion parameters (jump frequency and magnitude), Cornish-Fisher VaR adjustment, realized distribution fit test (Kolmogorov-Smirnov against assumed distribution).

3.4 Correlation & Dependency Structures

Definition: Measurement of linear and nonlinear dependencies between signals, instruments, and time periods. Hidden correlations are the primary source of unexpected blowups.

Metrics: Pearson correlation matrix (rolling 20-day) between all active signals, Spearman rank correlation (captures nonlinear dependency), tail dependence coefficient (correlation during extreme moves only), copula-based dependency modeling, signal redundancy score (percentage of variance explained by other signals), dynamic conditional correlation (DCC-GARCH).

3.5 Mean Reversion & Momentum Quantification

Definition: Statistical measurement of whether current price action exhibits mean-reverting or momentum-persistent behavior, and the strength of that tendency.

Metrics: Hurst exponent (H > 0.5 momentum, H < 0.5 mean reversion, H ≈ 0.5 random walk), variance ratio test (multi-scale), autocorrelation function at lags 1-20, half-life of mean reversion (Ornstein-Uhlenbeck parameter estimation), z-score relative to rolling mean, Augmented Dickey-Fuller test statistic.

3.6 Information Theory Metrics

Definition: Application of entropy and mutual information to quantify the predictive content of signals and detect regime transitions.

Metrics: Shannon entropy of return distribution (rolling), transfer entropy between signal and forward returns (directional causality), mutual information between candidate features and target variable, approximate entropy (ApEn) for complexity measurement, Kullback-Leibler divergence between current and historical return distributions (distribution shift detection).

SECTION 4: BEHAVIORAL FINANCE & CROWD PSYCHOLOGY

Markets are adversarial environments populated by participants with systematic cognitive biases. This domain quantifies those biases and translates crowd behavior into exploitable signals. The system does not trade psychology as narrative; it measures behavioral footprints with statistical precision.

4.1 Crowd Positioning & Sentiment Quantification

Definition: Aggregated measurement of where retail and institutional traders are positioned, using multiple independent data sources to triangulate consensus positioning.

Metrics: CFTC Commitment of Traders (COT) net positioning and rate of change, retail broker positioning data (where available), put/call ratio (equity and index options), social media sentiment score (NLP-derived from Twitter/Reddit/Discord, normalized to z-score), news sentiment polarity and volume, fund flow data (ETF inflows/outflows for QQQ/TQQQ/SQQQ), short interest ratio.

4.2 Fear & Greed Cycle Quantification

Definition: Measurement of emotional extremes that create systematic mispricing. Panic creates discounts. Euphoria creates premiums. Both are mean-reverting.

Metrics: VIX level and percentile rank, VIX term structure (contango/backwardation), VVIX (volatility of volatility), SKEW index, momentum exhaustion score (RSI divergence with volume confirmation), breadth divergence (advancing vs declining issues relative to index new highs), AAII/Investors Intelligence sentiment surveys, CNN Fear & Greed Index components.

4.3 Loss Aversion & Anchoring Effects

Definition: Measurable tendency of participants to anchor to entry prices, creating predictable support/resistance at psychologically significant levels and volume-weighted average entry zones.

Metrics: Volume Point of Control (VPOC) at session, weekly, and monthly scales, volume-weighted average entry price by time period, round number proximity (NQ 100-point levels), prior close as anchor, overnight high/low as anchor, VWAP as institutional anchor, trapped trader density estimate (volume above/below VWAP indicating underwater positions).

4.4 Herding & Momentum Cascade Detection

Definition: Identification of positive feedback loops where directional moves attract more participants, creating accelerating momentum that eventually exhausts and reverses.

Metrics: Order flow acceleration (delta rate of change), volume surge classification (panic vs conviction vs exhaustion), breadth participation rate, tick count divergence (number of up-ticks vs down-ticks relative to price), new high/low ratio velocity, options gamma exposure flip levels (where dealer hedging switches from dampening to amplifying moves).

Key Releases (Tier 2 — Conditional Monitoring): Durable Goods Orders, Housing Starts/Building Permits, Consumer Confidence (Conference Board & UMich), Industrial Production, Trade Balance, Treasury Auctions (2Y, 5Y, 10Y, 30Y).

Metrics: Pre-release volatility compression ratio, post-release volatility expansion magnitude and duration, surprise factor (actual vs consensus vs whisper number), directional bias by surprise direction, mean reversion timing post-release, spread widening magnitude and duration, false breakout probability in first 5 minutes post-release.

5.2 Interest Rate & Yield Curve Intelligence

Definition: NQ is among the most rate-sensitive equity indices. The yield curve encodes the market’s forward expectation of growth, inflation, and monetary policy, all of which directly drive tech stock valuations.

5.3 Cross-Asset Signal Matrix

Definition: Real-time correlation and lead-lag relationships between NQ and correlated instruments. Some cross-asset relationships are structural (NQ vs yields), others are regime-dependent (NQ vs USD), and the system must distinguish between them.

Key Relationships: NQ vs VIX (inverse, regime-dependent beta), NQ vs DXY (USD index; inverse in risk-on, positive in safe haven), NQ vs 10Y yield (inverse during rate-hiking, positive during growth scares), NQ vs crude oil (indirect via inflation expectations), NQ vs semiconductors (SOX/SMH as leading indicator), NQ vs mega-cap earnings momentum (AAPL, MSFT, NVDA, AMZN, META, GOOGL, TSLA concentration risk).

Metrics: Rolling correlation matrix (5-day, 20-day, 60-day), Granger causality test results (updated weekly), lead-lag optimal offset per pair, correlation regime classification (positive/negative/uncorrelated), correlation breakdown alerts (30-day correlation diverging from 90-day by > 1σ).

5.4 Options-Derived Intelligence

Definition: Information extracted from the options market that provides forward-looking insight into expected moves, positioning, and dealer hedging flows that directly impact NQ price action.

Metrics: Implied volatility term structure (front month vs back month IV), IV skew (25-delta put IV vs 25-delta call IV), gamma exposure by strike (GEX), dealer delta hedging thresholds, max pain strike, put/call open interest ratio by expiry, unusual options activity (large block trades in NQ options), zero-day-to-expiry (0DTE) options volume and gamma concentration.

5.5 Geopolitical & Black Swan Monitoring

Definition: Systematic monitoring of geopolitical events, policy shifts, and exogenous shocks that can produce regime transitions or tail events outside normal volatility models.

Metrics: Global Policy Uncertainty Index, geopolitical risk score (NLP-derived from wire services), sanctions/tariff announcement tracker, central bank communication sentiment (Fed, ECB, BOJ), pandemic/health crisis indicators, cyber attack frequency and severity index, supply chain disruption index.

SECTION 6: SESSION STRUCTURE & TIME-OF-DAY INTELLIGENCE

NQ futures exhibit strong, statistically significant time-of-day patterns in volatility, directional bias, mean-reversion tendency, and liquidity. Trading without this information is equivalent to ignoring a free edge.

6.1 Session Segmentation

Definition: Division of the 23-hour NQ futures trading day into distinct behavioral sessions, each with characteristic volatility, volume, and directional profiles.

Key Sessions: Asia (6:00 PM - 2:00 AM ET): Low volume, range-bound, liquidity vacuum risk. London (2:00 AM - 8:00 AM ET): Increasing volume, directional bias formation, liquidity sweeps of Asia range. NY Pre-Market (8:00 AM - 9:30 AM ET): Economic data releases, positioning adjustments, spread widening. NY Open (9:30 AM - 10:30 AM ET): Maximum volatility, highest volume, most false breakouts. NY Midday (10:30 AM - 2:00 PM ET): Lower volatility, mean-reversion dominant. NY Close (2:00 PM - 4:00 PM ET): Institutional rebalancing, renewed directional moves, MOC imbalance impact.

Metrics: Session ATR by session type, session directional bias probability, session open-to-close return distribution, session high/low formation time distribution, inter-session gap frequency and fill probability.

6.2 Time-of-Day Volatility Profile

Definition: Granular volatility mapping by 15-minute interval across the trading day, used to dynamically adjust stop placement, position sizing, and signal confidence.

Metrics: 15-minute ATR by time bucket (rolling 20-day), volume profile by time bucket, directional persistence by time bucket (autocorrelation), mean-reversion probability by time bucket, optimal holding period by entry time.

6.3 Day-of-Week & Calendar Effects

Definition: Systematic return and volatility patterns associated with specific days of the week, options expiration cycles, month-end rebalancing, and holiday effects.

Metrics: Day-of-week average return and volatility, OPEX week directional bias, triple/quadruple witching impact, month-end/quarter-end rebalancing flow estimates, holiday trading session characteristics, FOMC meeting week volatility expansion, NFP Friday pre/post-release volatility ratio.

SECTION 7: STRATEGY VALIDATION & ROBUSTNESS ENGINEERING

A strategy that cannot survive validation is not a strategy. It is a hypothesis that happens to fit historical data. This section defines the validation framework that every signal, parameter, and trading rule must pass before deployment. The standard is deliberately punishing. If a strategy fails here, it was never real.

7.1 Walk-Forward Analysis

Definition: Sequential train/test methodology where the model is trained on a rolling window of historical data and tested on the immediately following out-of-sample period. This is repeated across the entire available history.

Protocol: Minimum 3 years of tick data. 6-month training window, 1-month test window, sliding forward. At least 36 out-of-sample test periods. Walk-forward efficiency (OOS Sharpe / IS Sharpe) must exceed 0.5. If a strategy shows walk-forward efficiency below 0.3, it is overfit and must be discarded regardless of absolute return.

Metrics: Walk-forward efficiency ratio, out-of-sample Sharpe stability (coefficient of variation across test periods), parameter stability (optimal parameters should not shift more than 20% between adjacent windows), equity curve monotonicity, worst out-of-sample period drawdown.

7.2 Monte Carlo Simulation

Definition: Randomized resampling of trade sequences to estimate the distribution of possible outcomes given the strategy’s statistical properties, accounting for path dependency and drawdown risk.

Protocol: Minimum 10,000 iterations. Resample trade returns with replacement. Report: median terminal equity, 5th percentile terminal equity (worst realistic case), 95th percentile max drawdown, probability of ruin (equity reaching drawdown threshold), confidence interval for Sharpe ratio.

Metrics: Ruin probability (equity decline > 30%), median max drawdown, drawdown duration distribution, recovery time distribution, Sharpe confidence interval width.

7.3 Stress Testing & Adversarial Conditions

Definition: Deliberate degradation of execution quality and market conditions to identify the breaking point of each strategy.

Stress Scenarios: Double slippage (+2x average observed), signal delay (+500ms latency injection), spread doubling, volume drought (-50% average volume), flash crash replay (March 2020, Feb 2018 VIX explosion, Aug 2024 JPY carry unwind), correlated signal failure (all signals pointing same direction simultaneously).

Metrics: Breakeven slippage (maximum tolerable slippage before expectancy goes negative), maximum tolerable latency, strategy survival rate across historical tail events, max drawdown under each stress scenario, recovery time under stress.

7.4 Regime-Segmented Performance

Definition: Performance attribution segmented by market regime. A strategy with a 2.0 Sharpe that derives 100% of returns from trending markets and loses money in ranging markets is fragile. The system must know where its edge exists and where it does not.

Metrics: Sharpe ratio by regime (trending, ranging, volatile, chaotic), win rate by regime, expectancy by regime, profit factor by regime, maximum drawdown by regime, percentage of time active by regime, regime transition P&L (performance during regime shifts specifically).

7.5 Statistical Significance Testing

Definition: Formal hypothesis testing to determine whether observed strategy performance is distinguishable from random chance.

Metrics: t-statistic for mean return (must exceed 2.0 for significance), p-value for edge existence, bootstrap confidence intervals for Sharpe ratio, multiple comparison correction (Bonferroni or Benjamini-Hochberg) when testing multiple strategies simultaneously, White’s Reality Check for data snooping, deflated Sharpe ratio (accounting for number of strategies tested).

SECTION 8: RISK MANAGEMENT FRAMEWORK

Risk management is not a module. It is the operating system. Every other domain in this database is subordinate to risk control. The system’s first mandate is survival. Profit is a secondary objective that is only achievable if the system survives long enough for positive expectancy to compound. This section defines the non-negotiable risk boundaries.

8.1 Position Sizing Engine

Definition: Dynamic calculation of contract quantity per trade based on current equity, signal confidence, volatility regime, and correlation exposure.

Sizing Methods: Fixed fractional (baseline: 1% equity risk per trade), volatility-adjusted (Kelly fraction * ATR-normalized, with half-Kelly maximum), regime-adjusted (reduce size by 50% in chaotic regime, 25% in high-volatility regime), correlation-adjusted (reduce aggregate exposure when signal correlation exceeds 0.6).

Metrics: Current position size as percentage of equity at risk, volatility-normalized position size, effective leverage, portfolio heat (total open risk across all positions), margin utilization ratio.

8.2 Drawdown Control Hierarchy

Definition: Multi-layered drawdown limits that progressively reduce exposure as losses accumulate. This is a hard-coded kill switch, not a suggestion.

Hierarchy: Trade Level: Maximum loss per trade = 1% of equity. No exceptions. Daily Level: Maximum daily loss = 2.5% of equity. System halts all new entries for remainder of session. Weekly Level: Maximum weekly loss = 5% of equity. System enters observation-only mode for remainder of week. Monthly Level: Maximum monthly loss = 10% of equity. System halts for 48 hours, then resumes at 50% position size. Absolute Level: Maximum drawdown from equity peak = 20%. System enters emergency shutdown and requires manual review before reactivation.

Metrics: Current drawdown from equity peak, drawdown velocity (rate of loss), time in drawdown, drawdown recovery ratio (historical), current tier activation status.

8.3 Correlation Risk Management

Definition: Prevention of hidden concentration risk when multiple signals independently identify the same trade, creating an illusion of diversification while actually concentrating exposure.

Metrics: Real-time signal correlation matrix, effective number of independent bets (1 / sum of squared portfolio weights), maximum allowable exposure to any single directional thesis, cross-strategy position netting, tail correlation estimate (correlation during extreme moves specifically).

8.4 Liquidity Risk Management

Definition: Management of the risk that positions cannot be exited at acceptable prices due to market liquidity deterioration.

Metrics: Current bid-ask spread relative to historical average, order book depth relative to position size, estimated market impact of full position exit, maximum position size as percentage of average volume, liquidity drought detection (book depth < 50% of rolling average), circuit breaker proximity.

8.5 Tail Risk Hedging

Definition: Systematic protection against extreme adverse events that exceed normal volatility model assumptions.

Mechanisms: Dynamic stop adjustment based on ATR expansion, automatic position reduction when VIX exceeds 30, portfolio-level trailing stop (not just trade-level), options-based tail hedging framework (when capital permits), correlation to inverse volatility products monitoring, overnight exposure limits.

SECTION 9: EXECUTION INTELLIGENCE

The gap between a theoretical trading signal and actual P&L is execution. In NQ futures where average profitable trades measure in single-digit ticks, execution quality is not an optimization — it is an existential requirement.

9.1 Order Type Selection Engine

Definition: Dynamic selection between market orders, limit orders, stop orders, and conditional orders based on signal urgency, current spread conditions, and queue probability.

Decision Matrix: High urgency + tight spread = Market order (accept 0.5-1 tick slippage for certainty). Medium urgency + normal spread = Limit at best bid/ask (queue for fill, timeout to market if unfilled after threshold). Low urgency + wide spread = Passive limit inside spread (price improvement attempt, accept non-fill risk). Stop protection = Stop-limit with defined slippage tolerance (avoid stop-market in thin conditions).

Metrics: Fill rate by order type and market condition, average slippage by order type, queue position estimation accuracy, timeout rate for limit orders, partial fill frequency.

9.2 Latency & Infrastructure Monitoring

Definition: Continuous measurement of all latency components from signal generation through exchange acknowledgment, with automatic degradation detection and compensation.

Metrics: Signal computation latency (model inference time), order serialization latency, network round-trip time to exchange, exchange acknowledgment latency, total signal-to-fill time, latency spike detection (> 3σ from rolling mean), latency percentiles (p50, p90, p99, p99.9).

9.3 Smart Order Routing & Execution Algorithms

Definition: Algorithmic execution strategies that optimize fill quality beyond simple market/limit order submission.

Algorithms: TWAP (Time-Weighted Average Price) for large position entry/exit over defined window. Iceberg (display partial size, refill on execution) for large orders without signaling intent. Sniper (aggressive limit that chases price on favorable moves, waits on adverse moves). Peg-to-midpoint for spread capture during low-urgency entries.

9.4 Transaction Cost Analysis (TCA)

Definition: Comprehensive post-trade analysis of execution quality to identify systematic inefficiencies and improve the execution engine.

Metrics: Implementation shortfall decomposition (timing cost, market impact, spread cost, opportunity cost), execution quality by time-of-day, execution quality by volatility regime, execution quality by order type, broker/connectivity comparison (if multiple routes available), cost per round-trip in ticks and dollars.

SECTION 10: MONITORING, ADAPTATION & SELF-DIAGNOSTICS

A trading system that cannot detect its own degradation is a ticking time bomb. Edge decay is not a risk — it is a certainty. The only question is how quickly the system detects it and adapts. This domain provides the self-diagnostic framework that keeps the system alive as markets evolve.

10.1 Model Drift Detection

Definition: Continuous monitoring of signal efficacy to detect when a strategy’s edge is decaying due to market evolution, regime shift, or competitive erosion.

Metrics: Rolling Sharpe ratio (20-trade, 50-trade, 100-trade windows), rolling win rate vs historical baseline, rolling expectancy vs historical baseline, feature importance drift (are the same inputs still predictive), prediction residual distribution shift, CUSUM test for mean shift detection, Page-Hinkley test for change detection.

10.2 Performance Attribution Engine

Definition: Granular decomposition of P&L by every attributable dimension to understand where returns come from and where losses concentrate.

Attribution Dimensions: By signal type (which signals are generating alpha and which are decaying). By regime (trending, ranging, volatile, chaotic). By time-of-day (which sessions are profitable). By day-of-week. By trade duration. By entry quality (how much adverse excursion before reaching target). By execution quality (slippage contribution to P&L). By position size (are larger positions proportionally more or less profitable).

10.3 Automated Parameter Recalibration

Definition: Systematic, bounded adjustment of strategy parameters in response to detected market condition changes, within pre-defined safety constraints.

Safety Constraints: No parameter may move more than 15% from its walk-forward optimized baseline in any single recalibration cycle. Recalibration frequency: weekly maximum. All parameter changes are logged and compared to out-of-sample performance to measure recalibration effectiveness. If recalibration degrades performance for 3 consecutive cycles, revert to baseline and flag for human review.

10.4 System Health Dashboard

Definition: Real-time composite health score aggregating all system components into a single operational readiness assessment.

Components: Data feed health (latency, gaps, stale data detection), execution engine health (fill rates, latency, error rates), signal generation health (computation time, feature availability), risk engine health (position tracking accuracy, margin calculations), strategy health (current Sharpe relative to threshold), infrastructure health (CPU, memory, network, disk).

10.5 Anomaly Detection & Circuit Breakers

Definition: Automated detection of abnormal system behavior or market conditions that warrant immediate position reduction or trading halt.

Triggers: Data feed latency exceeding 3σ from rolling mean. Fill rate dropping below 80% for limit orders. Spread exceeding 3x rolling average for more than 30 seconds. Consecutive losing trades exceeding 99th percentile of historical sequences. Signal contradiction (long and short signals simultaneously active from independent systems). Exchange-level circuit breaker activation or proximity. Equity dropping through drawdown tier threshold.

SECTION 11: MACHINE LEARNING & ADAPTIVE MODELING

Machine learning is a tool, not a solution. The system employs ML only where it demonstrably outperforms simpler statistical methods on out-of-sample data, with full transparency into what the model has learned. Black-box models that cannot be interrogated are not permitted in production trading.

11.1 Feature Engineering Pipeline

Definition: Systematic construction, validation, and selection of input features from raw market data. Feature quality determines model quality. Garbage features produce garbage models regardless of architecture sophistication.

Feature Categories: Microstructure features (delta, depth imbalance, spread, trade flow). Price action features (distance from key levels, FVG proximity, order block proximity). Statistical features (ATR percentile, Hurst exponent, regime classification). Cross-asset features (yield change, VIX level, DXY move). Temporal features (time-of-day encoding, day-of-week, session flag). Sentiment features (NLP scores, COT positioning, put/call ratio).

Validation Requirements: Every feature must demonstrate statistically significant predictive power (mutual information > threshold) on out-of-sample data. Features must be computed in real-time with production-grade latency. Feature importance must be stable across walk-forward windows. Highly correlated features (r > 0.8) must be deduplicated to prevent multicollinearity.

11.2 Model Architecture Selection

Definition: Disciplined selection of model architectures based on the specific problem structure, not hype.

Permitted Architectures: Gradient Boosted Trees (XGBoost/LightGBM) for tabular signal combination and regime classification. These are the workhorse models. Interpretable, fast, robust. Logistic Regression with L1 regularization for simple binary signal combination where interpretability is paramount. Hidden Markov Models for regime detection. LSTM/Transformer networks permitted ONLY for sequence-dependent problems (order flow pattern recognition) with strict regularization and demonstrated OOS superiority over simpler alternatives. Reinforcement learning for execution optimization ONLY, not for signal generation (too prone to overfitting in non-stationary environments).

11.3 Model Validation Framework

Definition: Validation protocols specific to ML models that prevent the unique failure modes of learned systems (overfitting, data leakage, non-stationarity).

Protocols: Purged walk-forward cross-validation (gap between train and test to prevent leakage from overlapping labels). Feature importance stability analysis across folds. Permutation importance testing (does shuffling a feature meaningfully degrade performance). Adversarial validation (can a model distinguish train from test sets, indicating distribution shift). Calibration analysis (do predicted probabilities match observed frequencies). SHAP value analysis for prediction explanation.

11.4 Ensemble & Model Combination

Definition: Combination of multiple models to reduce variance and increase robustness, provided models are sufficiently independent.

Methods: Confidence-weighted averaging (models with higher recent accuracy receive more weight). Regime-switching ensemble (different models activate in different regimes). Prediction disagreement as uncertainty measure (when models disagree, reduce position size). Stacked generalization with purged cross-validation.

SECTION 12: DATA INFRASTRUCTURE & INTEGRITY

Data is the foundation. Corrupt data produces corrupt signals, which produce losses. There is no recovering from bad data downstream. Data integrity is an obsession, not a feature.

12.1 Data Sourcing & Validation

Primary Sources: CME direct market data feed (tick-level trades and Level II book data), economic calendar API (consensus, actual, revision data), options chain data (full strike/expiry matrix with Greeks), VIX and volatility surface data, cross-asset price feeds (bonds, USD, crude), sentiment data APIs (NLP-processed news and social media).

Validation Rules: Every incoming data point passes through a validation pipeline before entering the system. Timestamp monotonicity check (no out-of-sequence data). Price sanity check (no values outside 5σ of rolling mean without confirmation from multiple sources). Volume sanity check (no zero-volume bars during active session). Gap detection (missing bars flagged and interpolated with degraded confidence tag). Duplicate detection and removal. Timezone normalization to ET.

12.2 Storage Architecture

Design: Hot storage for real-time data (last 5 trading days) in memory-mapped structures for sub-millisecond access. Warm storage for recent historical data (last 90 days) in PostgreSQL with TimescaleDB extension for time-series optimization. Cold storage for full historical archive in compressed Parquet files on object storage. All data immutable once validated. No in-place updates. Corrections stored as new records with correction flags.

12.3 Data Pipeline Monitoring

Metrics: Feed latency per source, data gap frequency and duration, validation rejection rate (elevated rejection = source quality degradation), storage write latency, query response time, data freshness (time since last update per source), pipeline throughput (records processed per second).

SECTION 13: REGULATORY & COMPLIANCE AWARENESS

An institutional-grade system operates within regulatory boundaries. Non-compliance is an existential risk that no amount of alpha can offset.

13.1 Exchange Rules & Trading Limits

Key Rules: CME position limits for NQ futures, daily price limits and circuit breaker levels, pre-trade risk limits (max order size, max position), anti-spoofing and anti-layering compliance (order-to-trade ratio monitoring), wash trade prevention, audit trail requirements for all orders placed and cancelled.

13.2 Margin & Capital Requirements

Metrics: Initial margin requirement per contract, maintenance margin requirement, intraday margin (if applicable), portfolio margin benefit estimation, margin call proximity alert, overnight margin requirement differential, margin requirement as percentage of available equity.

13.3 Audit & Recordkeeping

Requirements: Complete order lifecycle logging (generation, submission, acknowledgment, fill, cancellation). Signal attribution for every order (which signal triggered the trade). Risk state at time of each order. System health state at time of each order. Immutable audit trail with tamper detection. Minimum 5-year retention.

APPENDIX A: RECOMMENDED INDEX STRATEGY

All tables partitioned by time use TimescaleDB hypertables. The following indexes are recommended for production query performance. Composite indexes are listed in selectivity order (most selective column first).

microstructure_metrics: PRIMARY on (ts, symbol); BTREE on (symbol, timeframe, ts); BRIN on ts for range scans.

price_action_zones: BTREE on (symbol, zone_type, ts_detected) WHERE ts_invalidated IS NULL (active zones only); BTREE on (validity_score DESC) for priority retrieval.

regime_classifications: BTREE on (symbol, ts DESC) for latest regime lookup; GIN on transition_probability for JSONB queries.

risk_snapshots: BTREE on (ts DESC) for current state; BTREE on (dd_tier_active) WHERE dd_tier_active != 'NORMAL' for alert queries.

execution_quality: BTREE on (trade_id); BTREE on (ts_signal, order_type) for TCA aggregation.

performance_attribution: BTREE on (signal_type, regime_at_entry, ts_entry) for segmented attribution queries; BTREE on (trade_id) for joins.

system_audit_log: BTREE on (event_type, ts DESC) for event retrieval; BTREE on (severity) WHERE severity IN ('CRITICAL','EMERGENCY') for alert queries; immutable — no UPDATE or DELETE permitted.

feature_store: BTREE on (symbol, feature_name, ts DESC) for latest feature lookup; partial index WHERE is_stale = FALSE.
